This research examines how long-distance ecological interactions reshape coastal wetland structure and resilience. Coastal landscapes are often organized by foundation species across elevation and inundation gradients, but these patterns are not determined by physical conditions alone. Biological interactions can operate over surprisingly long distances and alter habitat conditions across the landscape.

In the Yellow Sea coastal wetlands, we investigated how the invasion of *Spartina alterniflora* at low tidal elevations generates landscape-scale effects on native vegetation communities. By combining remote sensing, field observations, and ecological modeling, this work showed that an invasive foundation species can modify sedimentation and soil salinity, indirectly facilitating the expansion of *Phragmites australis* and contributing to the decline of native *Suaeda salsa* “Red Beach” marshes.

This study highlights that long-distance interactions can reorganize coastal landscapes and alter ecosystem resilience to future sea-level rise.

### Key questions

- How can species interactions operate across landscape scales?
- How do invasive foundation species reshape native coastal wetland structure?
- How do long-distance interactions influence ecosystem resilience under sea-level rise?

### Approach

- Remote sensing analysis of long-term vegetation change
- Field observations of vegetation, elevation, and soil conditions
- Ecological modeling of landscape transitions and resilience
