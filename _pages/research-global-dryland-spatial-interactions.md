---
title: "Quantifying spatial interactions and their resilience effects in global drylands"
permalink: /research/global-dryland-spatial-interactions/
author_profile: true
---

This research focuses on spatial interactions in global drylands and their consequences for ecosystem resilience. Dryland resilience is often interpreted through climate controls, water limitation, and local plant–soil feedbacks. However, dryland ecosystems are spatially extended systems, where vegetation change, sediment movement, habitat amelioration, and degradation processes can propagate across neighboring habitats.

A central question of this work is whether such spatial interactions can be systematically quantified at broad spatial scales, and whether they help explain why some dryland landscapes are more vulnerable to resilience loss than others. Using satellite-derived vegetation time series, I quantify the strength of cross-habitat spatial interactions across global drylands and examine how these interactions are associated with alternative vegetation states and vegetation recovery dynamics.

This work tests the idea that spatial interactions are not merely background spatial dependence, but an active ecological process shaping dryland stability. Strong interactions may homogenize neighboring habitats, reduce the local coexistence of alternative vegetation states, and slow vegetation recovery. In this sense, spatial interaction may act as an overlooked mechanism contributing to dryland resilience loss.

By linking spatial interaction strength with resilience indicators, this research aims to provide a broader view of dryland dynamics: local ecosystem changes are not isolated, but can be transmitted across space and scale up to influence landscape-level stability. This perspective may help improve the monitoring of dryland degradation risks and the identification of regions where spatially propagated feedbacks could accelerate ecosystem transitions.
