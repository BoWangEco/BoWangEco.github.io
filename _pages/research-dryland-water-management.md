This research investigates how human water-resource regulation shapes multistability and resilience in dryland ecosystems. Drylands are often viewed as systems constrained primarily by climate and natural water availability, but human activities can strongly modify ecosystem states by redistributing water, restoring vegetation, and altering feedbacks between vegetation, soil, and hydrology.

Focusing on the pan-Hexi Corridor in northwestern China, this work examines whether human-managed drylands can exhibit alternative stable states and how water-resource management influences ecosystem quality and resilience. Using a multi-source ecosystem-quality index, potential landscape analysis, resilience diagnostics, and regression models, we evaluate how natural water resources and human activities jointly shape ecosystem state transitions.

This study shows that human activity can improve ecosystem quality, but may also reduce resilience and increase dependence on sustained water inputs. It emphasizes the need to balance ecological restoration benefits with long-term water security in dryland management.

### Key questions

- Can human-managed drylands maintain multiple stable ecosystem states?
- How do water availability and human activities reshape dryland ecosystem quality?
- Does ecological water allocation improve ecosystem state while reducing resilience?
- How may future climate change alter regime-shift risks in dryland landscapes?

### Approach

- Multi-source ecosystem-quality assessment
- Potential landscape analysis of alternative stable states
- Resilience diagnostics along water-resource gradients
- Regression modeling of human activity and sectoral water use
- Future projection of ecosystem quality and regime-shift risk
